⚯ What am I looking at?

The Genesis Engine is an artificial-life substrate — a tiny universe whose only hand-written rules are physics. Everything else (creatures, species, predators, replicators) is meant to emerge, not to be programmed in.

It grew out of one question the artificial-life field keeps hitting: virtual life always stalls — it dies out, or it freezes into something static and stops surprising you. Real life complexified for four billion years. Nobody has solved this; it is the open problem. The Engine's honest take: build on a substrate that reliably produces living, moving creatures (Lenia), then layer on the four ingredients recent research (2022–2026) argues push toward open-endedness — and let you toggle each one to see what it does.

  • Lenia base — a continuous cellular automaton whose gliders (like Orbium) genuinely swim and collide. Start with the glider presets to see real motion. (Chan)
  • Embedded genome — each creature carries its own growth-rule genes (μ,σ) inside the medium, so they can mutate and drift. (Flow-Lenia parameter localization)
  • Metabolic economy — a light field becomes energy that gates growth; scarcity carves out niches. (Biomaker CA)
  • Mass conservation — the world's total matter is held near its seeded budget, so patterns compete instead of exploding or bleeding away. (Flow-Lenia)
  • Novelty drive (off by default) — watches the world and gives it a rare, gentle nudge only if it truly stalls. (inspired by ASAL, Sakana AI)

Try Glider collision to watch creatures interact, or Primordial soup to watch self-organization (and often, honestly, a freeze). Keep an eye out for 🐛 worms.
